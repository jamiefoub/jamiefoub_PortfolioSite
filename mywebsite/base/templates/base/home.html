DESIGN OF A CROSS DIE FOR SHEET METAL FORMING PROCESS
4th Year Project

I completed my 4th year individual project on the “DESIGN OF A CROSS DIE FOR SHEET METAL FORMING PROCESS”. In which I applied my Solid Works knowledge to produce CAD models of the complete DIE design as well as accompanying Solid Works component drawings. I was also required to learn and implement new skills in Abaqus FEA software to compute simulation testing of the DIE. I was graded an “A” for this work.

Abstract

Metal forming is the process of taking material that has already been simply formed from a shaping operation such as casting, melt extrusion or die casting and further forming it through plastic deformation into a more desired end shape. This end shape could be the final shape of the metal or to make it better for following processes, making it suitable for machining, metal treatment, joining, etc.

There are many types of metal forming from curling, bending, punching and hydroforming for instance. To apply these sheet metal forming techniques effectively it is important to understand how these forming techniques will affect the material being worked.

To get an idea of the limitations of the material and how it will perform, a number of simulations and experimental techniques can be used. However, these methods can prove to be quite expensive and rather time consuming. A more effective solution would be to design a cross-die. This thesis explores and proposes a design of a cross-die that will be used within the sheet metal forming process.

Aims and Objectives
  • Study background theory of sheet metal forming techniques to provide an understanding as to the purpose of sheet metal forming
  • Understand current sheet metal testing techniques and their possible shortcomings
  • Provide theory on Forming Limit Diagrams
  • Provide theory on cross-die testing and how it is an effective testing method
  • Showcase final cross-die design
  • Simulate cross dies testing in ABAQUS

DESIGN OF BLAST AND FIRE WALL FOR NEW BUILD FPSO
5th Year Project

For my 5th year group project, we were tasked with designing a fire and blast wall to separate modules on a new build FPSO, whilst optimising for a low cost and low weight design. This would provide protection between modules in the event of an explosion and accompanying jet fire.

My main contributions to the group were developing design optimisation code using MATLAB as well as producing the groups CAD models and drawings using Solid Works. I also carrying out the finite element analysis of the groups final blast wall design using Abaqus software. I was graded an “A” for this work.
